Concurrency in Golang

Friday, 21 August 2015

Table of Contents

  • Setting Up Go

Goroutines are like light weight threads. What makes the goroutine especially powerful is that it's a very light weight thread. What does that even mean? For a normal thread, the size of its stack (stuff where it stores its relevant variables) is around 1 MB. This means that we cannot have a ridiculous amount of them. But with a stack of around only 8 KB, we can have a lot of these goroutines, we can even use a single goroutine to handle a web socket connection. But goroutines alone aren’t enough to get things done. We need to have a way to get an update on these goroutines, we need to know what on earth they’re doing.

Most of go’s contemporary languages solve this problem by sharing chunks of memory. What does that even mean? Well, imagine we have two room mates, thready and threader. These guys love cheese cakes, in fact they buy a whole load of them and stuff it in their tiny little fridge. But what happens when thready eats a cheese cake? Threader will have to find out by coming back from his classes and taking a look at the fridge. In other words, both thready and threader have to check the fridge in order to see what the other is eating up. This is what sharing state basically is, you have a common place where information is stored, and all the threads check this store to see what's happening.

However, instead of this, thready can just send threader a message when he eats up one of their precious cheese cakes. In other words, they tell each other what's happening by communication through a medium. This is essentially what goroutines do using channels. They tell other goroutines anything worth mentioning.

This is what many developers (and with good reason) celebrate as sharing state through communication (what goroutines do) instead of communicating through sharing state. So, in this example, we are going to take a look at what these concurrency primitives can do for us, but first we need to get golang set up.
